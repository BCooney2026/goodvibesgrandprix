The Reason We Race

Free Childcare So A Parent Can Finish School

Every racer auctioned, every dollar bid, every laugh in that VFW hall on August 29 goes straight to keeping a parent in class and a child cared for.

Where It Starts

A few years ago, a young single mom stood in a Phoenixville Costco telling a friend she might have to drop out of college. Between a full course load, a job, and an infant daughter, the childcare bills alone were eating everything she had left after gas and diapers.

A stranger in line overheard her. She walked over and told her about a small charity a few miles away that provides free childcare to single parents working toward a degree. That conversation changed the trajectory of her family.

That's Mom's House. It has been the thing standing between "I have to quit" and "I graduated" for hundreds of Chester County parents since 1987, and it happens one overheard conversation, one referral, one open door at a time.

A Mom's House Story

First In Her Family To Go To College

A young mother from Perkiomen Valley wanted to be the first in her family to earn a degree. A teacher and school nurse connected her with Mom's House while she was still in high school. With free childcare covered, she stayed in class, kept her grades up, and years later walked across the stage at West Chester University, debt-free.

She calls Mom's House family. Today she works as a senior contract analyst and is raising three boys of her own.

It's Not Free-For-Nothing

A Hand Up, Not A Handout

Every Mom's House parent holds up their end of the deal. This isn't charity that asks for nothing back, it's a partnership that expects effort:

  • Attend school full time, and keep grades passing
  • Volunteer two hours each week at Mom's House
  • Attend monthly parenting and life-skills workshops

In exchange, their children ages 3 months to 5 years get a state-licensed classroom, hot meals, and a second family who shows up for them every single day. That combination, accountability plus real support, is why Mom's House graduates go on to become nurses, teachers, analysts, and homeowners instead of statistics.

What Your Bid Actually Buys

Mom's House gets zero federal or state funding. Every diaper, every meal, every teacher's paycheck comes from people who decided to show up, people exactly like the crowd at the Good Vibes Grand Prix.

A $10 opening bid on a racer is a week of a toddler's supplies. A hard-fought bidding war is a semester of tuition-adjacent breathing room for a mom who is one bad week from dropping out. This is the whole point of the race.
